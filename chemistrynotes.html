Chapter 1: Discovery of Electron, Proton and Neutron

Discovery of Electron (J.J. Thomson, 1897)

  • Discovered using a discharge tube containing gas at very low pressure (~10⁻⁴ atm) under high voltage (~10,000 V).
  • Cathode rays were emitted from the cathode and traveled toward the anode.
  • These rays were deflected by both electric and magnetic fields toward the positive plate — proving they consist of negatively charged particles.
  • Thomson calculated the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of the electron = 1.76 × 10⁸ C/g.
  • The e/m ratio was found to be the same regardless of the gas used in the tube — proving electrons are a universal constituent of all atoms.

Discovery of Proton (E. Goldstein, 1886)

  • Discovered through canal rays (anode rays) — positively charged rays moving from anode toward cathode, observed through holes drilled in the cathode.
  • Unlike cathode rays, the e/m ratio of canal rays varies with the gas used in the discharge tube.
  • The smallest and lightest positive particle was obtained when hydrogen gas was used — named the proton by Rutherford (1919).
  • Charge on proton = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C; Mass ≈ 1.6726 × 10⁻²⁴ g (1 amu).

Millikan's Oil Drop Experiment (1909)

  • Tiny oil droplets were sprayed into a chamber and allowed to fall under gravity between two charged plates.
  • By adjusting the electric field, droplets could be held stationary — balancing electric force against gravity.
  • Found charge on electron = −1.6022 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (the fundamental unit of charge).
  • Using e/m (from Thomson) and e (from Millikan), mass of electron = 9.11 × 10⁻²⁸ g (about 1/1837th the mass of a proton).

Quick Revision

  • Cathode rays = electrons (same e/m for all gases). Canal rays = positive ions (e/m varies with gas).
  • Smallest positive particle = from H₂ gas = proton.
  • Millikan ⇒ charge on electron. Thomson ⇒ e/m ratio. Combine both ⇒ mass of electron.
  • Common trap: Don't confuse Thomson (e/m) with Millikan (e) — a frequent MCQ distractor.

Chapter 2: Cathode Rays & Canal Rays Properties

Properties of Cathode Rays

  • Travel in straight lines (cast sharp shadows of objects placed in their path).
  • Travel perpendicular to the surface of the cathode, regardless of anode position.
  • Possess kinetic energy — can rotate a small paddle wheel placed in their path (proves they have mass and momentum).
  • Deflected by electric and magnetic fields → confirms they are charged particles, not electromagnetic waves.
  • Can ionize gases through which they pass and cause fluorescence on striking ZnS (zinc sulphide) screens.
  • Produce X-rays when they strike a heavy metal target (links Ch.2 to Ch.7).
  • Independent of the electrode material and the gas in the discharge tube.

Properties of Canal Rays (Anode Rays)

  • Travel in straight lines, in a direction opposite to cathode rays.
  • Deflected by electric/magnetic fields toward the negative plate, confirming positive charge.
  • Speed is much less than that of cathode rays (because positive ions are much heavier than electrons).
  • e/m ratio depends on the nature of the gas — heavier gas gives a smaller e/m ratio.
  • Composed of positively charged ions formed when cathode ray electrons knock electrons off gas molecules.

Quick Revision

  • Cathode ray properties that prove particle nature: paddle wheel rotation (mass) + deflection (charge).
  • Cathode rays → independent of gas/electrode. Canal rays → dependent on gas.
  • Exam trick: "Cathode rays produce X-rays on striking heavy metals" frequently links Ch.2 to Ch.7.

Chapter 3: Discovery of Neutron (Chadwick's Experiment, 1932)

Background Problem

  • Atomic mass of elements (except hydrogen) was roughly double the atomic number — the nucleus contained mass not accounted for by protons alone.
  • This "missing mass" puzzle led scientists to predict a neutral particle in the nucleus.

Chadwick's Experiment

  • Bombarded a beryllium (Be) target with high-energy alpha particles (α) emitted from polonium.
  • Reaction: ⁹Be + ⁴He(α) → ¹²C + ¹n
  • Emitted radiation could penetrate thick sheets of lead but was not deflected by electric or magnetic fields — proving it carried no charge.
  • When the radiation struck paraffin wax (rich in hydrogen), it knocked out high-speed protons.
  • By conservation of momentum, Chadwick calculated mass ≈ mass of a proton → named neutron.
  • Neutron: neutral charge, mass ≈ 1.0087 amu (slightly greater than proton's mass).

Quick Revision

  • Target = Beryllium. Bombarding particle = Alpha particle. Result = Neutron.
  • Neutron mass is slightly greater than proton mass — common MCQ distractor.
  • Key proof: high penetration + no deflection in fields = neutral particle.
  • Neutron discovered ~30 years after electron/proton — explains why Rutherford's and Bohr's models had no neutron.

Chapter 4: Rutherford's Atomic Model & Its Defects

Gold Foil (Alpha Scattering) Experiment

  • A beam of fast-moving alpha particles was directed at an extremely thin sheet of gold foil (~10⁻⁴ cm thick), surrounded by a circular ZnS fluorescent screen.
  • Observation 1: Most alpha particles (~99%) passed straight through → atom is mostly empty space.
  • Observation 2: A small fraction were deflected at various angles → a concentrated positive region exists.
  • Observation 3: ~1 in 8000 bounced almost straight back (~180°) → this region is extremely small but contains nearly all the mass.

Postulates of Rutherford's Model

  • All positive charge and almost all mass is concentrated in a tiny central nucleus.
  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular orbits — similar to planets orbiting the sun ("planetary model").
  • Nucleus size (~10⁻¹³ cm) is extremely small compared to atom size (~10⁻⁸ cm) — atom is mostly empty space.
  • Number of electrons = number of protons → atom is electrically neutral.

Defects of Rutherford's Model

  • Stability problem: An orbiting electron must continuously emit radiation, losing energy, and spiral into the nucleus within ~10⁻⁸ seconds — but atoms are stable. Model fails to explain this.
  • No explanation for line spectra: Continuous energy loss predicts a continuous spectrum; but atomic spectra show discrete line spectra.
  • No electron arrangement: Model gives no concept of shells or energy levels.

Quick Revision

  • Mnemonic for 3 observations: "Mostly empty, slightly deflected, rarely bounced back."
  • Biggest defect = instability (spiraling electron) — directly fixed by Bohr's Postulate 3.
  • Nucleus : Atom size ratio ≈ 1 : 10,000 — frequently asked.
  • Trap: Rutherford's model did NOT include the neutron (discovered 1932, after 1911 model).

Chapter 5: Bohr's Atomic Theory & Postulates

Postulates of Bohr's Theory

  • Postulate 1 (Quantized Orbits): Electrons revolve only in certain fixed circular paths called orbits/shells/energy levels, designated by principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3...
  • Postulate 2 (Angular Momentum Quantization): mvr = nh/2π
  • Postulate 3 (Stationary States): Electrons in a given orbit do not radiate energy — these are called stationary/ground states. Directly resolves Rutherford's stability defect.
  • Postulate 4 (Energy Absorption/Emission): ΔE = E₂ − E₁ = hν

Key Formulas (Hydrogen-like species)

  • Radius of nth orbit: rₙ = (0.529 × n²)/Z Å
  • Energy of nth orbit: Eₙ = −(13.6 × Z²)/n² eV
  • For H atom (Z=1), n=1: radius = 0.529 Å, energy = −13.6 eV

Hydrogen Spectral Series

  • Lyman series — falls to n=1 (UV region)
  • Balmer series — falls to n=2 (Visible region)
  • Paschen series — falls to n=3 (Infrared region)

Limitations of Bohr's Theory

  • Could not explain spectra of multi-electron atoms (only works for H and H-like ions: He⁺, Li²⁺).
  • Could not explain Zeeman effect (splitting in magnetic field) or Stark effect (splitting in electric field).
  • Violated Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle — assumes exact position and velocity simultaneously known.
  • Did not consider the wave nature of electrons (de Broglie).
  • Could not explain fine structure of spectral lines (later explained by Sommerfeld's elliptical orbits).

Quick Revision

  • Postulate that fixes Rutherford's defect: Postulate 3 (stationary states).
  • mvr = nh/2π — memorize; directly tested.
  • Eₙ = −13.6/n² eV for H: E₁=−13.6, E₂=−3.4, E₃=−1.51 eV.
  • Series energy order: Lyman > Balmer > Paschen (Lyman = highest energy, shortest wavelength).
  • Top AFNS trap: Bohr fails for multi-electron atoms — most tested limitation.

Chapter 6: Planck's Quantum Theory

Planck's Postulates (1900)

  • Energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously — it comes in discrete packets called quanta (photons for light).
  • Energy of one quantum: E = hν, where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck's constant).
  • Total energy: E = nhν (n = integer).
  • Since c = νλ: E = hc/λ — energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.

Significance

  • Resolved the "ultraviolet catastrophe" — classical physics predicted infinite energy at short wavelengths from a hot black body.
  • Provided the basis for Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect.
  • Became the foundation of Bohr's 4th postulate (ΔE = hν).
  • Marked the birth of Quantum Mechanics.

Quick Revision

  • E = hν = hc/λ — most repeated formula across the entire syllabus.
  • Energy ∝ frequency (direct); Energy ∝ 1/wavelength (inverse). Classic trick question reverses these.
  • h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s — memorize value and units.
  • Chain: Planck → Einstein (photoelectric) → Bohr (postulate 4). "Which came first?" is a favorite MCQ.

Chapter 7: X-Rays & Atomic Number (Moseley's Law)

Discovery & Production of X-Rays

  • Discovered by Wilhelm Roentgen (1895) — accidentally, while working with a cathode ray tube.
  • Produced when high-speed cathode ray electrons strike a heavy metal target (e.g., tungsten or molybdenum).
  • X-rays are electromagnetic radiation of very short wavelength (~0.01 to 10 nm) — very high energy and penetrating power.
  • Not deflected by electric or magnetic fields (uncharged).

Moseley's Law (1913)

  • Studied X-ray spectra of various elements; found a relationship between X-ray frequency and the atomic number (Z) of the target.
  • Mathematical form: √ν = a(Z − b), where 'a' and 'b' are constants (b ≈ 1).
  • A plot of √ν vs Z gives a straight line.
  • Proved that atomic number (Z), not atomic mass, is the fundamental property determining an element's position in the periodic table.
  • Corrected the periodic table (resolved Co/Ni, Ar/K anomalies) and predicted undiscovered elements.

Quick Revision

  • Moseley's Law: √ν = a(Z − b) — directly examinable formula.
  • Biggest significance: atomic number (not mass) is the basis of periodic classification.
  • X-rays produced by decelerating fast electrons hitting a metal target — link back to Ch.2.
  • Trap: X-rays are NOT charged (unlike cathode/canal rays) — hence not deflected by fields.

Chapter 8: Quantum Numbers

1. Principal Quantum Number (n)

  • Denotes the main energy level/shell in which an electron is located.
  • Values: n = 1, 2, 3, 4... (K, L, M, N shells).
  • Determines size of the orbital and most of the energy.
  • Maximum electrons in a shell = 2n².

2. Azimuthal Quantum Number (l)

  • Denotes the subshell/shape of the orbital within a shell.
  • Values: l = 0 to (n−1).
  • l=0 → s (spherical); l=1 → p (dumbbell); l=2 → d (cloverleaf); l=3 → f (complex).

3. Magnetic Quantum Number (m)

  • Describes the orientation of the orbital in 3D space.
  • Values: m = −l to +l (total of 2l+1 values).
  • s: 1 orbital; p: 3 orbitals; d: 5 orbitals; f: 7 orbitals.

4. Spin Quantum Number (s)

  • Describes the spin direction of the electron.
  • Values: +½ (↑) or −½ (↓) only.
  • Each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins.

Quick Revision

  • n → shell/size/energy; l → subshell/shape; m → orientation; s → spin.
  • For n=4: n² = 16 orbitals → max 32 electrons (2n²).
  • Number of orbitals in a subshell = 2l+1 (s=1, p=3, d=5, f=7) — heavily tested.
  • Trap: m values depend on l, NOT directly on n.

Chapter 9: Electronic Configuration Rules

1. Aufbau Principle

  • Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy — lowest energy fills first.
  • Order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d...
  • (n+l) rule: Lower (n+l) fills first; if tied, lower n fills first. E.g., 4s (n+l=4) before 3d (n+l=5).

2. Pauli Exclusion Principle

  • No two electrons in an atom can have the same set of all four quantum numbers.
  • Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons with opposite spins.

3. Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity

  • Electrons in degenerate (equal energy) orbitals occupy separate orbitals singly with parallel spins before any pairing occurs.
  • Example: C (2p²) → ↑ in 2p_x and ↑ in 2p_y (not paired in same orbital).

Exceptions to Aufbau Principle (High-Yield)

  • Chromium (Cr, Z=24): Expected [Ar]3d⁴4s² → Actual = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ (half-filled d gives extra stability).
  • Copper (Cu, Z=29): Expected [Ar]3d⁹4s² → Actual = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ (fully-filled d gives extra stability).
  • Reason: Half-filled and fully-filled subshells have greater symmetry and exchange energy stabilization.

Quick Revision

  • 4s fills BEFORE 3d (Aufbau) — but when writing ion configs, 4s electrons are removed first. E.g., Fe²⁺ = [Ar]3d⁶. Very high-yield trap.
  • Cr = [Ar]3d⁵4s¹ and Cu = [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹ — memorize these cold; appear almost every year.
  • Pauli: max 2 e⁻/orbital opposite spins. Hund: distribute before pairing in same subshell.

Chapter 10: Modern Periodic Table & Shielding Effect

Modern Periodic Law

  • "Physical and chemical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers" — direct consequence of Moseley's work.
  • 7 horizontal rows (periods) and 18 vertical columns (groups).
  • 4 blocks: s-block (Groups 1-2), p-block (Groups 13-18), d-block (Groups 3-12), f-block (lanthanides & actinides).

Shielding (Screening) Effect

  • Inner-shell electrons repel/shield outer (valence) electrons from the full nuclear pull.
  • Reduces the net positive charge experienced by valence electrons = Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff).
  • Formula: Zeff = Z − S (Z = atomic number, S = shielding constant).
  • Across a period: Shielding ~constant, Z increases → Zeff increases → atomic size decreases.
  • Down a group: More shells added, shielding increases significantly → atomic size increases.

Quick Revision

  • Period number = number of electron shells = highest n for that element.
  • Zeff = Z − S; greater shielding → lower Zeff → larger atomic size, lower ionization energy.
  • Key link: Shielding is the master concept behind Chapters 11, 12, 13.

Chapter 11: Ionization Energy & Electron Affinity

Ionization Energy (IE)

  • Minimum energy required to remove the outermost electron from an isolated, neutral gaseous atom in its ground state.
  • Always positive (endothermic).
  • Across a period: IE generally increases (increasing Zeff, decreasing size).
  • Down a group: IE generally decreases (increasing size and shielding).

Exceptions to IE Trend (High-Yield)

  • Be > B: Be has stable filled 2s²; B loses a higher-energy 2p¹ electron more easily.
  • N > O: N has stable half-filled 2p³; O's paired 2p⁴ electron is easier to remove due to repulsion.
  • Period 3 analogs: Mg > Al and P > S (same reasons).

Electron Affinity (EA)

  • Energy change when an electron is added to a neutral, gaseous atom to form an anion.
  • Usually exothermic (negative) for non-metals.
  • Across a period: EA magnitude increases (higher Zeff). Down a group: EA magnitude decreases.
  • Exception: Cl > F in EA magnitude — F's tiny 2p orbitals cause high e⁻-e⁻ repulsion when a new electron is added.
  • Noble gases have EA ≈ 0 or slightly positive (added electron must enter a new higher-energy shell).

Quick Revision

  • Memorize 4 IE exceptions: Be>B, Mg>Al and N>O, P>S — all due to full/half-filled stability.
  • EA exception: Cl > F in magnitude — most commonly tested EA exception.
  • IE = always positive (endothermic). EA = usually negative (exothermic) except noble gases.

Chapter 12: Electronegativity & Metallic Character

Electronegativity (EN)

  • Relative tendency of a bonded atom to attract the shared pair of electrons toward itself.
  • Most common scale: Pauling scale — Fluorine (F) = 4.0 (highest of all elements).
  • Across a period: EN increases (increasing Zeff, decreasing size).
  • Down a group: EN decreases (increasing size and shielding).
  • Least electronegative = most electropositive = Cesium/Francium (bottom-left).

Metallic Character

  • Tendency of an atom to lose electrons and form cations — related to low IE and low EN.
  • Across a period: Metallic character decreases.
  • Down a group: Metallic character increases.
  • Most metallic = bottom-left (Cs, Fr). Most non-metallic = top-right (F, excluding noble gases).

Quick Revision

  • Fluorine = most electronegative (4.0) — one of the most repeated single facts in chemistry MCQs.
  • Metallic character is the OPPOSITE trend to electronegativity and ionization energy.
  • Noble gases: technically excluded from EN comparisons — their full octet gives them near-zero tendency to share electrons.

Chapter 13: Atomic Radius & Ionic Radius Trends

Atomic Radius

  • Half the distance between the nuclei of two bonded identical atoms (covalent radius).
  • Across a period: Atomic radius decreases — same shells, increasing Zeff pulls electrons closer.
  • Down a group: Atomic radius increases — additional shells added, shielding increases.

Ionic Radius

  • Cations: Always smaller than the parent atom (electrons removed → less repulsion, tighter pull, often lose an entire shell).
  • Anions: Always larger than the parent atom (electrons added → more repulsion, lower Zeff per electron, cloud expands).

Isoelectronic Species

  • Species with the same number of electrons.
  • Among isoelectronic species: more protons (higher Z) = smaller radius.
  • Example (all 10 e⁻): O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺ (Z: 8→13, radius decreases).

Quick Revision

  • Cation always smaller, anion always larger than the parent atom — one of the most-tested facts.
  • Isoelectronic: same electrons → more protons = smaller radius.
  • This chapter ties together Chapters 10, 11, 12 — expect "which property follows the same/opposite trend" MCQs.
